Motion Architecture Decision Records (ADR) Guide

Documenting motion decisions — not just implementing them — keeps a design system consistent and maintainable as teams, requirements, and browsers change over time.

What is a Motion ADR?

Definition

A record documenting a specific decision about how motion is implemented, standardized, or constrained within a project.

Purpose

Captures the reasoning behind a motion choice at the time it was made, not just the resulting code.

Benefits

Prevents relitigating settled decisions and gives future contributors context they'd otherwise have to guess at.

Typical Use Cases

Standard timing scales, reduced-motion strategy, token naming, browser support policy, and animation deprecation.

Why Teams Need Motion ADRs

Consistency

Ensures every team applies the same timing and easing conventions.

Accessibility

Documents how reduced-motion and vestibular concerns were addressed.

Browser Compatibility

Records which browsers and fallback strategies were considered.

Performance

Explains why certain properties or techniques were chosen or avoided.

Team Collaboration

Gives distributed teams a shared reference instead of tribal knowledge.

Long-Term Maintenance

Lets future contributors understand intent before changing behavior.

Motion ADR Lifecycle

  1. 1

    Identify Decision

    Recognize a motion choice significant enough to warrant a record.

  2. 2

    Gather Context

    Collect constraints, requirements, and prior related decisions.

  3. 3

    Evaluate Alternatives

    Compare viable options and their trade-offs explicitly.

  4. 4

    Approve Decision

    Reach consensus with relevant stakeholders before implementation.

  5. 5

    Implement

    Apply the decision consistently across the affected codebase.

  6. 6

    Review

    Confirm the decision holds up once implemented and in use.

  7. 7

    Archive or Update

    Mark superseded decisions clearly rather than deleting their history.

Standard ADR Template

Status
Proposed, Accepted, Superseded, or Deprecated.
Context
The circumstances and constraints that led to this decision being needed.
Problem
The specific motion-related problem being addressed.
Decision
The choice that was made, stated clearly and unambiguously.
Alternatives
Other options considered and why they were not chosen.
Consequences
The resulting trade-offs, both positive and negative.
Future Considerations
Conditions under which this decision should be revisited.

Example Motion ADRs

ADR-001 Standard Motion Timing

Problem: Duration values were inconsistent across components.

Decision: Adopt a fixed duration scale (150/300/500ms) for all transitions.

Reasoning: A small, named scale is easier to apply consistently than arbitrary values.

Outcome: New components now reference the scale instead of hardcoding durations.

ADR-002 Reduced Motion Strategy

Problem: prefers-reduced-motion was handled inconsistently per component.

Decision: Centralize reduced-motion handling in a shared utility layer.

Reasoning: Removes reliance on individual developers remembering to add it.

Outcome: All animated components now inherit reduced-motion behavior by default.

ADR-003 Motion Token Naming

Problem: Motion-related custom properties had no consistent naming convention.

Decision: Prefix all motion tokens with --motion- followed by a semantic name.

Reasoning: Improves discoverability and prevents collisions with unrelated tokens.

Outcome: Token usage is now searchable and self-documenting across the codebase.

ADR-004 Browser Support Policy

Problem: No clear policy existed for adopting experimental CSS motion features.

Decision: Require @supports-based fallbacks for any feature without universal support.

Reasoning: Progressive enhancement keeps the baseline experience functional everywhere.

Outcome: New features ship with a documented fallback path from day one.

ADR-005 Animation Deprecation

Problem: Deprecated animation classes remained in use with no migration path.

Decision: Deprecated classes must be documented with a replacement and removal date.

Reasoning: Gives consumers advance notice instead of silent breaking changes.

Outcome: Deprecation notices are now tracked alongside the changelog.

Best Practices

Tip

Keep ADRs concise — a page or less is usually enough.

Tip

Record trade-offs explicitly, not just the final decision.

Tip

Link related ADRs so decisions can be traced together.

Tip

Review ADRs periodically as requirements and browsers evolve.

Tip

Version decisions rather than editing history silently.

Common Mistakes

Warning

Missing rationale — recording what was decided without explaining why.

Warning

Recording implementation details instead of the decision itself.

Warning

Ignoring rejected alternatives, losing valuable context for later reviewers.

Warning

Never revisiting old ADRs as the project and its constraints change.
